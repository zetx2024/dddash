Formulating a Research Question

The Significance of Research Questions

The research question (RQ) holds immense importance in crafting your academic papers. Allow us to highlight the significance of RQ through the following key points:

I. Focus and Direction

It helps define the boundaries and scope of the research, guiding the researcher’s efforts toward a specific objective. A solid research question would enable researchers to concentrate on a narrowed-down topic.

II. Purpose and Significance

Additionally, a research question articulates the purpose and significance of the study. It highlights the problem or gap in knowledge that the research aims to address. The question clearly clarifies why the matter is worth studying.

III. Testability and Measurability

A research question should always be testable and measurable. A clear research question allows for the formulation of specific research hypotheses, facilitating the identification of variables, data collection methods, and statistical analyses necessary to answer the question.

Formulating a Research Question

So now, how can you start formulating your own research question?

I. Subject of Interest

To develop a research question, you need to first identify your subject of interest and then do preliminary research on that subject. Your subject of interest should be narrowed down later to find a specific question to grasp.

II. Specificity, Novelty, and Relevance:

Ensure your research question is specific, novel, and relevant. Avoid broad or overly general questions and focus on a specific aspect or relationship. Consider the feasibility, ethical considerations, and alignment with research objectives.

Criteria for a Respected Research Question

The previous three characteristics are essential for your research question to be respected. However, there are other tremendously crucial and essential characteristics to take into consideration. Now, you will be learning about the most handy criteria for making a respected research question. Those criteria are SMART, FINER, and FINERMAPS.

CriteriaExplanation
FINER- Feasible: Is the research question realistic and achievable within the available resources, time, and constraints?

- Interesting: Does the research question address a relevant and significant issue in the field?

- Novel: Does the research question contribute new insights or knowledge to the existing literature?

- Ethical: Does the research question adhere to ethical principles and guidelines?

- Relevant: Is the research question directly related to the research objectives and the scope of the field?
SMART- Specific: Is the research question clearly defined and focused on a particular aspect or relationship?

- Measurable: Can the research question be answered through observable and measurable data?

- Achievable: Is the research question realistically attainable within the available resources and time frame?

- Relevant: Does the research question align with the research objectives and address a significant gap or issue?

- Time-bound: Is there a specific timeline or deadline associated with the research question?
FINERMAPS- Feasible: Is the research question feasible in terms of available resources, time, and access to data or participants?

- Interesting: Does the research question address an interesting and relevant issue in the field?

- Novel: Does the research question contribute new knowledge or insights to the existing literature?

- Ethical: Does the research question comply with ethical guidelines and considerations?

- Relevant: Is the research question directly relevant to the research objectives and aligned with the study’s scope?

- Measurable: Can the research question be answered through measurable and observable data?

- Appropriate: Are the research methods and design appropriate for addressing the research question?

- Parsimonious: Is the research question formulated in a concise and straightforward manner?

- Significant: Does the research question have the potential to generate significant findings?

Refining the Research Question

I. Preliminary Reading and Narrowing Down

Conduct preliminary reading to identify topical debates and issues in your subject area. Narrow down a specific niche or focus area within the broader topic.

II. Seeking Feedback and Revising

Seek feedback from colleagues, mentors, or experts in the field to refine and improve your research question. Be open to revising and refining as you gain new insights during the research process.
