Essential Software for the Journey

Types of Programs

In regard to the research process, there are 3 main types of programs that can offer substantial help:

I. Simulation and Field-Specific Programs:

These include programs like AutoCAD, Gambit, Aspen Plus, and other programs that help you make mathematical models, conduct experiments in simulations, or even make visual models and schematic diagrams for your paper. The recommended programs differ greatly according to your research field. Other factors, such as the budget and level of experience needed, can significantly affect your best choice.

This training guide does not include detailed explanations and tutorials for each program due to the existence of thousands of them for varying needs. Nevertheless, at YRJ, we highly recommend that you contact your mentor regarding possible software options designed for your field. They will suggest resources for you to know the process of downloading and using these software programs.

II. Research Assistant Tools:

These are generic tools that automate some steps of the research process. This includes citing sources, proofreading, and correcting grammatical and stylistic errors. You can sort items into collections and create full bibliographies using these tools in a few minutes.

III. Typesetting Software:

After collecting the data needed for your research paper and forming your original arguments, you will consider presenting your work in the most accurate and professional shape and format. While Word offers a variety of features and flexibility as a text editor, it has limitations when style consistency is considered, which makes it difficult to maintain a united style throughout your paper. Moreover, writing long mathematical proofs, code sections, and other technical text is challenging in Word since it was designed with the ‚ÄúOffice‚Äù needs in mind but not the ‚ÄúResearch‚Äù needs. Hence, configuring your document settings and formatting it appropriately can take precious hours from your time. Thus, while Word and Google Docs may seem quick options, you should consider professional software such as LaTeX and Scrivener if you want to maximize your time efficiency on the long run.

Zotero

Zotero is an open-source research assistant tool that helps you manage your bibliographies and make automatic citations. Zotero is a valuable tool that can help you organize your sources and also find articles you need on the internet. Use this video to help you get started.

Features of Zotero include:

  • Collaborating with a colleague while writing a paper or sharing sources.
  • Collecting metadata and citation information through the browser extension.
  • Automatically creating and organizing a bibliography and a reference list for your paper.
  • Support for experience-enhancing plugins. The official documentation allows you to delve deeper into the program capabilities and maximize your efficiency while using it.

Plagiarism Checkers:

Plagiarism Checkers are an essential tool to use before submitting your paper. After completing each section of your paper, you should put the text in a plagiarism checker to ensure that you did not accidentally plagiarize. A plagiarism checker scans your text and compares it with papers stored in a large database to analyze the similarities between your text and other sources. LaTeX (optional)

LaTeX is a distribution used for the production of high-quality technical documents, especially research papers. LaTeX is much better than other writing software like Microsoft Word for producing complex documents. This is due to the availability of a vast library that includes customizable elements that allow users to change the format of their papers easily. It also includes built-in graphing software for displaying data neatly. LaTeX comes in many distributions.

LaTeX Distributions

  1. Basic LaTeX + Notepad (or any text file generator): That is true. If you have downloaded LaTeX from the official website. You can have access to it right away. Open any text file and write your LaTeX text and then save it as a .tex file. The distribution includes a very basic PDF converter so that you can export your text and review your work. However, this option does not have many features compared to the next options since it is time consuming and generally considered outdated for both beginners and professionals.
  1. TeXStudio and other desktop distributions: TeXStudio is a standard choice if you need a desktop distribution that runs on Windows, Unix/Linux, BSD, or Mac OS X. Besides, if you are new to LaTeX, then TeXStudio can help you learn quickly through its visually rich GUI and menus for the different symbols and formatting functions. TeXStudio is an open-source software. Hence, there are no limitations on what you can do. It is also installed with a What You See is What You Get (WYSWYG) option for newcomers.
  1. Overleaf: Overleaf is an online LaTeX distribution. Hence, it does not require any setup and works perfectly fine regardless of the operating system. Moreover, Overleaf offers many exclusive features, including free access to thousands of professional templates, online collaboration, and improved console and error detection. However, the standard version of Overleaf has a processing time limit of 1 minute for your documents. This means that if the compiler takes more than 1 minute to process your document, it will not be converted into PDF. Hence, it may be difficult to maintain a large document on Overleaf.

The easiest distribution for LaTeX is Overleaf. You can find a concise and nice guide on the website to get started quickly.

Basics of LaTeX

Writing your first LaTeX document

A basic LaTeX document will just consist of a plain page with a paragraph in it. The LaTeX structure for this is as the following:

\documentclass{article}
\begin{document}
First document. This is a simple example, with no
extra parameters or packages included.
\end{document}

Notice that any LaTeX command is preceded by a backslash ‚Äú\‚Äù. The first line \documentclass{article} determines the type of our document to be an article. There are other types of document classes such as books, letters, slides, and beamer. However, you will be mostly working with articles. The \begin{document} and its end tag mark the space in which you will be writing your paper.

Adding more stuff to our plain paper

Suppose I want to give our paper a title and an author, maybe even a date. I also want the font size to be 14pt. Then it can be done this way:

\documentclass[14pt]{article}
\title{Your first document}
\author{The author‚Äôs name}
\date{June 2022}
\begin{document}
\maketitle
First document. This is a simple example, with no
extra parameters or packages included.
\end{document}

I put the size of the font I want in square brackets in the \documentclass[14pt] tag. The title of the paper and its author‚Äôs name are written in the tags specified for them. However, they won‚Äôt be displayed until you write the command \maketitle inside the document begin and end tags.

Text Formatting

These are several tags you should know for formatting your text:

\textbf{your text here will be bold.}
\underline{underlined text.}
\textit{italicized text.}
\textbf{\textit{italicized and bold text.}

Creating Lists

You can create an unordered list using the following block:

\begin{itemize}
\item The individual entries are indicated with a black dot, a so-called bullet.
\item The text in the entries may be of any length.
\end{itemize}

Where every entry in the list must be preceded by an \item tag.

For ordered lists, we use enumerate inside of the itemize tag:

\begin{enumerate}
\item This is the first entry in our list
\item The list numbers increase with each entry we add
\end{enumerate}

Creating Chapters and sections

The method for this is straightforward just use the tags \chapter{your chapter‚Äôs name here} and \section{section name here}:

\begin{document}
\chapter{First Chapter}
\section{Introduction}

Here you write your introduction and let‚Äôs say you are finished and want to go to another section.

\section{Discussion}

You can also divide sections into subsections:

\subsection{subsection1}
\subsection{subsection2}
\end{document}

Making a bibliography

To cite anything neatly in your paper you can make a bibliography using the following command:

\begin{document}
%Suppose this is the line you want to cite in your paper \cite{Name of the textbook/paper you cited}.
\begin{thebibliography}
\bibitem{Name of the textbook/paper you cited}
%Here you insert the Harvard format citation for the book/paper. You can get it from google scholar or citation machine. For example:
Shannon, A. (2012). Elliptic Tales: Curves, Counting and Number Theory, Princeton University Press
\end{thebibliography}
\end{document}
