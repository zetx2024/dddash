How to Excel in Competitions: Knowing the Right Competitions

How to Choose the Best Competition for You?

Choosing the best competition for you can depend on several factors, including your interests, skills, and goals. Here are some steps you can take to help you determine the best competition for you:

  • Identify your interests: Consider what topics or activities you enjoy and what motivates you. For example, if you enjoy writing, look for writing competitions.
  • Assess your skills: Think about what you are good at and what you can contribute to the competition. If you have strong public speaking skills, look for a competition that involves presenting speeches.
  • Determine your goals: Consider what you want to achieve by participating in a competition. Do you want to win a prize, learn something new, or gain recognition?
  • Research the competitions: Look for competitions that align with your interests, skills, and goals. Check out their websites, read the rules and requirements, and learn about the prizes and judging criteria.
  • Consider the level of competition: Determine if the competition is suitable for your skill level. Some competitions are geared towards beginners, while others require advanced skills.
  • Check the deadline: Make sure you have enough time to prepare your submission or participation before the deadline.
  • Seek advice: Ask for recommendations from your teachers, mentors, or peers who may have participated in similar competitions before.

By considering these factors and doing your research, you can find the best competition that fits your interests, skills, and goals.

Types of Research Competitions Based on the Field of Study

There are several types of research competitions that offer a great opportunity to engage in pursuing their research in their field of interest.

The most famous types of those competitions are:

  • Natural science
  • Medical studies and healthcare
  • Engineering and technology
  • Humanities
  • Agriculture studies
  • Social science

Research Competition Types Based on the Methods of the Competition.

Science Explanation Competitions. 

These competitions ask students to communicate a scientific concept in an interesting and accessible way, typically through a short video. The goal is for participating students to “learn by teaching” while also spreading their knowledge and enthusiasm for science with other teens.

Science Research Competitions. 

One of the most well-known types of science competitions for high school students is the science research competition. These competitions involve students using the scientific method to investigate a question of their choice about the natural world. (The scientific method is a systematic process that includes making observations, formulating a question or hypothesis, conducting research, analyzing data, and drawing conclusions.) Students then present their findings either in person (at a science fair, in the form of a project or display) or by submitting a research paper. Science research competitions are usually held at the local, regional, or state level and are organized by schools, science clubs, professional societies, or science industry organizations.

Science Knowledge Competitions. 

These competitions, also known as Olympiads or bowls, are exam-based competitions designed to challenge students’ academic and technical knowledge of various STEM subjects, such as biology, chemistry, physics, or computer science. These competitions are held on a single day or weekend and, in addition to tests, can include a variety of events such as lab experiments and hands-on building activities. They are organized by state and national organizations and held at different levels, from invitational to regional, state, and national. In some cases, national winners go on to compete in international competitions.

Science Writing Competitions. 

These competitions ask students to explore and comment on an aspect of science’s role in society. Like other types of science competitions, students must understand the scientific principles behind what they are writing about, but they must also go further and analyze, critique, or advocate for an element of science based on how they think it affects the world.

Science Application Competitions. 

Applied science competitions offer students the chance to demonstrate their skill in the practical, real-world application of a STEM area – usually a form of engineering – such as computer programming, robotics, or rocketry. Because the topics of applied science competitions aren’t typically taught in high schools, these competitions usually involve a learning component guided by a teacher or other adult mentor who supervises a team of students. Applied science competitions are often organized as multi-level tournaments (similar to science knowledge competitions) where the winning teams at each level (regional and state) move on to compete for a national or international title.

Science Innovation Competitions. 

These competitions are designed to promote innovation and may take the form of hackathons, business plan competitions, or design challenges. The goal is for students to develop and present new ideas, prototypes, or business plans that have the potential to solve real-world problems such as environmental conservation, energy efficiency, or disease/disability.

Competitive Research

Definition

Competitive research, also known as competitive analysis, is the process of gathering and analyzing information about your competitors in order to better understand their strengths, weaknesses, and strategies. The goal of competitive research is to gain insights that can help you make informed decisions about your own business or product strategy, such as identifying opportunities for growth, differentiating yourself from competitors, or addressing areas of weakness.

Steps for making a do well in the competitive research

I. Identify main competitors

The simplest approach to do this is to just perform a web search for your product or service category to see what results are returned. Additionally, you can look at Crunchbase and Product Hunt websites. You can come across rivals that you hadn't previously noticed.

Casting a wide net will help you identify who your primary rivals are. Asking your target clients what services they now use is another effective technique to uncover direct and indirect competition.

II. Gather information

Acting like one of your competitors' consumers is the best approach to learn about them. Join their email list to learn more about their communication style.

Observe how they engage with their clients online by following their blog and social media pages. Customers' experiences with your rivals: what kind?

You should think about purchasing from them so you can see what their goods looks like and how the consumer experience is.

III. Track findings

To aid in continued monitoring, make sure you keep note of your competitors' findings on a spreadsheet. It's not a difficult process; all you have to do is observe what they do over time to see how they alter everything from price to marketing and promotional initiatives.

You'll begin by creating separate columns for your direct and indirect competitors' customers. Then, you'll keep tabs on the following data:

  • Company name
  • Website
  • Social media sites
  • Unique features
  • Pros and cons
  • Screenshots and additional links

IV. Check online reviews

As many reviews of your rivals as you can locate are recommended. Read the case studies on their website, blog comments, and social media reviews. Read the Google reviews as well, if they provide them. Understanding both the positive and negative aspects of what your competitors are doing is a smart idea. In your study, be sure to cite any mentions of them by the Better Business Bureau.

How attentive to customers are they? You might have a chance to make a statement here. Additionally, if they sell a product comparable to yours, this will be a useful technique to determine whether a large number of people are interested in it.

Any negative feedback will help you identify areas where you can improve your own product or service.

V. Identify areas for improvement

It's time to consider how you can use this information to better your own business performance now that you've noted some of the key distinctions between you and your rivals.

Your competition analysis should identify at least one area where your company might use some improvement. This will teach you how to interact with your clients and online followers more effectively.

Recall that a competitive research project is never a "one-and-done" activity. For you to remain competitive in the market, ongoing monitoring is required, such as watching how competitors change.

How to Lead a Team in Research Project?

In addition to the motivation they will receive from being lauded and honored for their accomplishments, research project competitions are important teamwork exercises that help students develop their scientific and creative mindsets. I recently served as the team leader for a group that won awards at the Hong Kong ICT Awards as well as the Asia-Pacific Information and Communications Technology Alliance.

It takes time and careful planning to successfully manage a research project competition for academics. In particular, students' ongoing involvement and motivation are essential for success. As a result, when managing such a project, it's crucial - but difficult - to get everyone on the team to share the same level of passion and dedication to the goals. This guide offers guidance on how to steer a varied group of students towards project achievement.

Challenges of project supervision

The main difficulties you will probably face while supervising a research project are the following, from the initial choice of a target area to subject research, topic execution, monitoring, outcomes, evaluation, and conclusions:

  1. Incentives, motivation and teamwork: To attract talented students to commit to a project competition, well-balanced incentives that are perceived to fairly recognize student contribution and individual effort are needed. This can be challenging. Students tend to lose some of their interest in and dedication to a project when facing difficulty and/or failure. Also crucial is the creation and nurturing of a motivated, hard-working team, and for this the elimination of potential freeloaders is critical.
  1. Student capability: Each student has their own abilities and strengths, which might include domain-specific technology, management skills or social networking. Identifying and leveraging these can be difficult, especially for long-term competitions.
  1. Project coordination: Conflicts and disputes often occur at various stages of a competition, including during ideation, execution, preparation, group presentations and even during the award-winning stages. In order for effective project coordination, the supervisor needs to play a leading role and take charge in the event of disagreement.
  1. Time management: Many students find it hard to cope with their academic studies while also devoting themselves to a project competition. This can lead to undesirable outcomes that could include withdrawal from the project or study probation.

Bringing students on board

A team that includes both undergraduates and postgraduates with a range of skills and abilities is often the result of a combination of voluntary participation and suggestions among students. For instance, the team from Hong Kong Polytechnic University (PolyU) that conducted the successful research project "Mutual Cognitive Human-Robot Collaborative Manufacturing System" included both undergraduate and graduate students.

In this instance, one undergraduate team member was recommended to join the project because of their excellent performance in an interdepartmental final-year project, and another highly motivated student volunteered to take part through the university's undergraduate research and innovation program. Two PhD students were added to the group in consideration of their areas of specialization, and their skills complemented those of the other team members.

Understanding and anticipating student concerns

Common student concerns over participating in such a competition often include the following: what benefits can I obtain from the project? What are the consequences if I cannot meet the supervisor’s expectations? How much time do I need to spend on this project? Why is the project important for my future study and career?

It is therefore useful to prepare for such questions, and others, in terms of having a clear idea of how to sell the positives to a busy student who might be on the fence about taking part. When subsequently briefing students, it’s important for them to reaffirm their dedication for the entirety of the project and seek departmental and other supervisors’ approval, as well as for you to ensure your own expectations and those of the students align.

Finding the right research topic

In devising a research topic, it’s advisable to leverage any support that can be provided by expert facilities on campus. In addition, an understanding of government policy and state-of-the-art research trends can certainly help align a team’s research needs. In our case, the Research Group of AI for Industrial Digital Servitisation was already established for industrial technology research. Therefore, the team’s research topic, which centered on collaborative human-robot manufacturing systems, fitted in with both an established research facility on campus and with government policy to promote industrial technology.

Ensuring effective time management

Effective time management plays a key role in determining whether students can handle their own academic study and project duties at the same time. To this end, you might use the “stage gate” project management method. This breaks down large projects into a number of stages or “gates”. When the team reaches the next gate, the work carried out on it is reviewed before a decision is made on whether the project is ready to move to the next stage. This is an excellent way not only to monitor progress but ensure quality.

Making adjustments

To be able to effectively adjust the progress of a project, from the start it is useful to apply a management model for control and continual improvement. The four-stage Plan-Do-Check-Act (PDCA) cycle, can be useful here:

  • Planning: Adopt a long-term vision and goal when devising a plan and program of adjustment for the project. A supervisor’s primary role is to focus on macro issues and ensure the project is progressing on the right track.
  • Testing: Conduct a pilot study and, based on preliminary results, make appropriate changes.
  • Checking: Review test results conducted by students and identify a critical path or workflow for them to follow.
  • Target-oriented action: Set concise, clearly defined goals for students to achieve, along with specific deadlines and relevant deliverables.

Efficient research project management creates an environment within which effective teamwork, constructive sharing and meaningful collaboration together comprise the drivers for achieving the desired outcome. Even for a science research project, adopting a business management process for solving problems and managing change is highly useful for keeping students motivated and engaged, and ultimately for project success.
