It is usually a brief of typically 150-300 words that is designed to give an overview of your entire project, in particular its findings and contribution to the field. Literature Review

This section is considered your warehouse source to your own arguments in the paper. Basically, it is a collective section for all of your precious work you have been searching for. Literature Reviews help readers understand what is known about a topic without having to find and read through multiple sources. In addition, they provide you helpful background and context. Literature reviews can also help you learn about a given topic while in the process of preparing the review itself. In the act of research and writing the literature review, you gain expertise on the topic. Pursuing to formulate a research paper, you should make use of every word you read throughout your journey of research, the reason why the Literature Review section is such important to formulate following these guidelines:

I. Identify and define the topic you will be reviewing

The topic, which is commonly a research question (or problem) of some kind, needs to be identified and defined as clearly as possible. You need to have an idea of what you will be reviewing in order to effectively search for references and to write a coherent summary of the research on it. At this stage it can be helpful to write down a description of the research question, related area, or topics that you will be reviewing, as well as to identify any keywords that you will be using to search for relevant research.

II. Conduct a literature search

Use a range of keywords to search databases that may contain relevant articles. You should focus on peer-reviewed and scholarly articles. Published books may also be helpful, but keep in mind that peer-reviewed articles are widely considered in scientific research. Read through titles and abstracts, select and obtain articles (that is, download, copy, or print them out), and save your searches as needed.

III. Read through the research you have gathered and never forget to take notes

Absorb as much information as you can. Read through the articles and books that you have found, and as you do, take notes. The notes should include anything that will be helpful in advancing your own thinking about the topic and in helping you write the literature review (such as key points, ideas, or even page numbers that index key information). Some references may turn out to be more helpful than others; you may notice patterns or striking contrasts between different sources; and some sources may refer to yet other sources of potential interest. This is often the most time-consuming part of the review process. However, it is also where you get to learn about the topic in great detail.

IV. Create an outline

At this stage, you are close to writing the review itself. However, it is often helpful to first reflect on all the reading that you have done. What patterns stand out? Do the different sources converge on a consensus? Or not? What unresolved questions still remain? You should look over your notes (it may also be helpful to reorganize them), and as you do, do think about how you will present this research in your literature review. Are you going to summarize or critically evaluate all this harvest? Are you going to use a chronological or other type of organizational structure?

V. Write the literature review itself (what you have written) and edit and revise as needed

The final stage involves writing. When writing, keep in mind that literature reviews are generally characterized by a summary style in which prior research is described sufficiently to explain critical
findings but does not include a high level of detail (if readers want to learn about all the specific details of a study, then they can look up the references that you cite and read the original articles themselves).¬† However, the degree of emphasis that is given to individual studies may vary (more or less detail may be warranted depending on how critical or unique a given study was). After you have written your draft, you should read it carefully and then edit and revise as needed.

VI. Incorporate the final Literature Review to your paper draft

After the literature review is complete, you should incorporate it into your research paper (if you are writing the review as one component of a larger paper), if not, just submit it where it is required.

Data Collection methods:

Quantitative methods Research: This methodology is for a research that relies on measuring variables using a numerical system, analyzing these measurements using any of a variety of statistical models, and reporting relationships and associations among the studied variables.

Techniques of quantitative methodology include:

  • Experiments

    Share full details of the tools, techniques, and procedures you used to conduct your experiment.

    • How did you design the experiment?
    • How did you recruit participants (if required)?
    • How did you manipulate and measure the variables (Set them at first)?
    • What tools did you use?
    • What was your time frame?
    • What were some failures or negatives you faced and could tackle?
  • Analysis of existing data

    Explain how you gathered and selected the material (such as datasets) that you used in your analysis.

    • Where did you source the material?
    • How was the data originally produced (somehow a literature review, but less detailed)?
    • What criteria did you use to select material (did you have a check list?)?
  • Surveys

    Describe where, when, and how you have conducted the survey.

    • How did you design your questionnaire?
    • What form did your questions take (MCQ, short answers, etc.)?
    • Were your surveys conducted in-person or virtually?
    • How did you choose your study participants?
    • How is your participants sample set range classified?
    • What was your sample set size and response rating criteria?

Qualitative methods Research: This methodology is for a research that produces descriptive (non-numerical) data, thus it‚Äôs often more flexible and subjective despite the status of the quantitative methods generally.

Techniques of qualitative methodology include:

    • How long will your experiment be?

Mixed methods research: This type of data collection combines quantitative and qualitative methods altogether. It‚Äôs usually considered when it is no more sufficient to just rely on either the quantitative or the qualitative alone, thus you became in need to have a combined argument from both types. Following this mixed technique, it is more likely to end up your methods exhibiting a strong, integral approach to what have been done with a net persuasive conclusion for the entire procedure.

This is the fruit of your paper, in other words, it is the place where you present your study findings in the most appropriate way you can ever have. Whatever the way you choose to present your study findings, you should always consider these points while articulating the context of your results section:

  • Deal with the Results section the same you did with the conclusion, making it expressive on its own, with no need to have additional section to understand its context.
  • Present your data coupled with a statement description for an easier clarification.
  • Be concise, using non-textual elements, such as figures and tables, if appropriate, to present results more effectively.
  • You are not required to prove anything, thus stay unbiased and present the entire sets of data you have without omitting or manipulating any subset.
  • You may rely on some non-textual additions like the figures, tables, charts, etc.
  • Do never forget to mention(in numbered order) the non-textual additions you supplied within the textual content of your results (Table 1, Chart 2, Map 3, etc).

  • Title of document or article
  • Title of complete work (if applicable), italicized

General guidelines

  • Organize your Reference page by the last names of the authors (or editors).

  • Use a hanging indent, so that the first line is at the margin and all following lines are indented.
  • If a work has no author or editor, alphabetize by the first word of the title other than a, an, or the.
